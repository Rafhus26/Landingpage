What Is a USDA Loan?

Zero Down Financing for Eligible MA Homebuyers

A USDA loan is a government-backed mortgage guaranteed by the U.S. Department of Agriculture through its Single Family Housing Guaranteed Loan Program. Despite the "rural" label, many towns and suburbs in Massachusetts qualify — often surprising buyers who assumed only remote areas were eligible. The program is designed to help moderate-income households achieve homeownership in less-densely populated communities.

USDA loans come with income limits based on household size — generally up to 115% of the area median income for your county. This means you don't have to be low-income to qualify; a household earning a solid middle-class wage can often still fall under the limit. I'll run a quick eligibility check for your specific town and household size before we even get started.

One common misconception is that USDA loans come with Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI). They don't. Instead, the program uses a guarantee fee structure: a one-time upfront guarantee fee of 1% of the loan amount (which can be financed into the loan) and an annual fee of 0.35% — significantly lower than FHA's annual mortgage insurance premium. On a $350,000 loan, the annual USDA fee amounts to roughly $97/month, versus $161/month for a comparable FHA loan.

Why Choose USDA

Key Benefits of a USDA Loan

For eligible buyers, USDA financing is often the most cost-effective mortgage option available in Massachusetts.

💵
Zero Down Payment

Keep your savings intact. USDA loans require no down payment, allowing you to finance 100% of the purchase price. This is one of only two zero-down programs available (the other being VA loans for veterans).

📈
Below-Market Interest Rates

Because USDA loans carry a government guarantee, lenders can offer highly competitive interest rates — often matching or beating FHA rates, and sometimes approaching conforming conventional rates.

💰
Low Monthly Fees

The USDA annual guarantee fee of 0.35% is considerably lower than FHA's annual MIP. Over the life of a loan, this difference can add up to thousands of dollars in savings for the borrower.

🏠
Geographic Flexibility

Many Massachusetts suburban towns qualify — including communities in Worcester County, Hampshire County, and beyond. Towns like Shrewsbury, Grafton, Northborough, and dozens of others may be eligible. I'll verify your target area instantly.

🛡
No PMI

USDA loans do not require Private Mortgage Insurance. The guarantee fee structure replaces PMI and costs significantly less than what you'd pay on an FHA loan or a conventional loan with less than 20% down.

📋
Fixed Rate Stability

USDA guaranteed loans are exclusively available as 30-year fixed-rate mortgages. Your principal and interest payment stays the same for the life of the loan — no rate surprises, no adjustment caps to track.

Do You Qualify?

USDA Loan Requirements in Massachusetts

USDA eligibility comes down to two main factors: your income and the location of the property.

Income & Credit
  • ✓Credit score of 640+ recommended for streamlined underwriting (lower scores considered with manual review)
  • ✓Household income must be at or below 115% of the area median income for your county and household size
  • ✓Stable, consistent employment history — typically 2 years in the same field or with the same employer
  • ✓Debt-to-income ratio generally under 41%; up to 50% with strong compensating factors
  • ✓All household members' income counts toward the income limit — not just the borrowers'
Property Eligibility
  • ✓Property must be located in a USDA-eligible rural or suburban area (check eligibility map at usda.gov)
  • ✓Must be your primary residence — vacation homes and investment properties do not qualify
  • ✓Single-family homes, eligible condominiums, and some manufactured homes qualify
  • ✓Property must pass a USDA appraisal and meet minimum property standards
  • ✓No acreage limits, but property must be primarily residential in nature
Common Questions

USDA Loan FAQ

More Massachusetts towns qualify than most people expect. USDA eligibility is based on census population data and is updated periodically. Many towns in Worcester County, Hampshire County, Franklin County, and parts of Middlesex County are eligible. Towns like Grafton, Northborough, Boylston, Holden, Sterling, Paxton, and many others in central and western MA typically qualify. Notably, densely populated cities like Boston, Worcester, Springfield, and Lowell are generally not eligible — but their surrounding suburbs often are. The best way to verify is to check the official USDA eligibility map at eligibility.sc.egov.usda.gov or let me run the check for you in minutes.
USDA income limits are set at 115% of the area median income and vary by county and household size. For example, in Worcester County, a household of 1-4 people may have a limit around $112,450–$120,000 (limits are updated annually). Larger households get higher limits. Importantly, all income from all adult household members counts — not just the borrowers on the loan. Certain deductions (childcare expenses, disability costs, dependent deductions) can reduce your counted income. I can run a full income eligibility analysis for your specific household before you apply.
If you qualify for both, USDA is almost always the better financial choice. USDA requires zero down payment versus FHA's 3.5%. USDA's annual fee (0.35%) is lower than FHA's annual MIP (0.55%+). USDA rates are often competitive with FHA rates. The primary advantages of FHA are that it has no income limits, no geographic restrictions, and more flexibility for lower credit scores. If you're buying in an eligible area and your household income is under the USDA threshold, USDA is typically the more affordable long-term option. I'll run side-by-side payment comparisons for you so you can see the actual dollar difference before you decide.
